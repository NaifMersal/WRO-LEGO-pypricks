The fix, in code

def gyro_turn(target):                        # def = name a move once, use it forever
    hub.imu.reset_heading(0)
    left.run(200); right.run(-200)            # fast for the long haul…
    while hub.imu.heading() < target - 30:
        wait(5)
    left.run(50); right.run(-50)              # …crawl the last 30°
    while hub.imu.heading() < target:
        wait(5)
    left.stop(); right.stop()

gyro_turn(90)     # or gyro_turn(45), gyro_turn(180)…

Two speeds, two whiles: fast for the long haul, crawl the last 30° — momentum arrives with nothing left to steal. And line 1: def names the move once; every call replays it.

First def of the bootcamp — land it hard: a function is a named move, exactly like run_angle is someone else’s named move. This version handles right turns (positive targets); if a kid asks about left turns, brilliant — flip the two run signs and the < to >, or park it as a challenge. The constants 200/50/30 are the raw material the afternoon interrogates; RobotConfig at D10. Demo on the next slide — walk the highlights here first.

D. Name It: the P-controller

You’ve been running this controller your whole life. Time to write it down.

You already run this controller 🚶

The game

Stand three big steps from the wall. Walk to it and stop with your toes exactly on the line. Round 1: normal. Round 2: robot style — full speed, then slam the brakes at the end. Round 3: crawl the entire way. Which round felt right — and what were your legs doing in it?

Your legs already run the deal: far → fast · close → gentle · there → zero. No zones, no steps — your speed simply follows the distance left. Robots can sign the same deal.

⏱️ Time: 5 minutes

Same unplugged-first habit as the D3 inner-ear game. Round 2 reproduces the coast (they’ll lean past the line or stutter-brake); round 3 is the sleepy 45. Ask round-1 walkers to narrate their own speed out loud — “fast… slower… slower… stop” — that sentence is the controller.

Say the deal in numbers

Target 90°, heading reads 20°. Call the degrees-left the error — here, 70. Build the wheel speed from the error: you’re allowed one multiplication.

What does your rule give at error 70? At 10? At 0?

speed = error × gain  (gain = 4)
error 70 → speed 280  flat out 🚀
error 10 → speed 40    tiptoe 🐢
error 0  → speed 0     parks itself 🎯

speed = error × gain — the whole staircase, in one line. gain is the slope: how hard to push per degree still to go. And at error 0 the speed is 0 — it doesn’t need to be told to stop.

Elicit the multiplication — don’t reveal it. Most squads propose “speed = distance left” first; ask “what if the wheels need bigger numbers?” and the × gain falls out. The three sample rows are the wall-walk in numbers: narrate them as “fast… slower… stop”, same sentence as the game.

The proportional turn

def gyro_turn_p(target, gain=4):
    hub.imu.reset_heading(0)
    while abs(target - hub.imu.heading()) > 1:   # until within 1°…
        error = target - hub.imu.heading()       # degrees still to go
        left.run(gain * error)                   # far → fast
        right.run(-gain * error)                 # close → gentle
        wait(5)
    left.stop(); right.stop()

gyro_turn_p(90)   # gyro_turn_p(45)… gyro_turn_p(180)… gyro_turn_p(-90)!

Line 10: a negative target flips the sign, the wheels swap — left turns come free. Yesterday’s version needed surgery for that.

Walk the highlight stops: gain arrives as a parameter with a default — new syntax, teach it in one breath (“a knob you can leave alone”). Line 3’s abs() is what makes negative targets work. Demo on the next slide.

Coach aside — “why run() and not motor.dc()?” (for the sharp student who’s read ahead). Honest truth: run() is itself a closed-loop speed controller inside Pybricks — so this hand-written P sits on top of it, a controller-on-a-controller. Your gain sets a speed target; Pybricks’ own loop chases it. A pro writing a controller from scratch often reaches for motor.dc() instead — raw power/duty — so their formula is the whole controller, one layer, nothing hidden underneath. Totally legitimate, and purer. We keep run() on purpose for two reasons: it’s smoother and battery-proof (a fresh vs. tired hub turns the same — priceless with five robots on the bench), and it speaks the same “speed” language as drive.drive(speed, turn) and DriveBase — so speed = error × gain stays literally true all the way from this turn, through the self-healing straight, to D7’s line-follower. dc() is the right tool the day you build a controller with no help underneath; that day isn’t this bootcamp.

Quick quiz: the gain knob 🚨

Before you touch the robot: gain = 1 — what will the turn feel like? gain = 20? Predict both.

1 = lazy — near the target it pushes too weakly to beat friction and parks short. 20 = hot — it flies past and zig-zags back. Tuning is the middle: hard enough to arrive, soft enough to arrive calm. That lazy leftover it parks on gets a name tomorrow. 😏

The prediction is the day’s real gate: “the bigger the error, the harder the push” plus what too-small/too-big does. Collect predictions out loud, then feel the answers — flick back to the playground and drag the gain slider to 1 and 20, or better, straight onto the bench robot in the lab. Per the plan, tuning this by feel without prompting is the strongest robotics-instinct signal on the scorecard — watch for who reaches for the knob before you suggest it.

Talk it out

Heading says +8 — nosed right. You command each wheel’s speed. What should the wheels do about it?

And should a +2 error get the same medicine as a +20 error?

Steer back left — right wheel faster, left slower — until heading reads 0. And the bigger the error, the harder the push.

The drive that heals itself

hub.imu.reset_heading(0)
left.reset_angle(0)

while left.angle() < 1304:        # until ≈ 100 cm rolls by…
    error = hub.imu.heading()     # +8 = nosed right · -5 = nosed left
    left.run(500 - 4 * error)     # nosed right? left eases off…
    right.run(500 + 4 * error)    # …right pushes harder → steers back left
    wait(10)
left.stop(); right.stop()

Run it — and nudge it again. 😈

It fights back. A hundred times a second: read the error, steer against it. Error 0 → wheels equal → dead straight. That tiny loop is the heart of every self-driving machine on Earth.

Check the signs with them, don’t just assert: error +8 → left runs 468, right runs 532 → right faster → robot swings left → error shrinks. Negative error works out symmetrically. The 4 is a strength knob — let one squad try 1 (lazy) and 20 (jittery zig-zag). Name it out loud: the same P-controller as this morning’s turn — one formula, second application; the third is D7’s line-follower. Same run()-is-a-speed-controller caveat as the proportional-turn note applies here — the 500 ± 4*error is a base speed plus a proportional trim, and it stays on run() for the same reasons (smooth, battery-proof, speaks DriveBase’s “speed” language).

F. Competition & Gate

Today’s showdown: Nudge Wars 💥

Nudge Wars 💥

The challenge

Drive 1.5 m straight while the judge nudges your robot twice mid-run. Score = closest final heading to 0° + straightest trace. 3 attempts, best counts.

  • Tune on the bench first — your straight’s 4 is a knob too: too soft and a shove sends you wandering; too hot and the trace zig-zags all the way down the lane.
  • Watch one recovery closely: the harder the shove, the harder the fight-back. The shove is the error.

Format: a 1.5 m taped lane; the judge nudges at roughly the 0.5 m and 1.0 m marks with a consistent finger-push to the robot’s side. Final |heading| is the score; trace straightness (eyeball the wheel-path) breaks ties. Uses only today’s material: error × gain on the straight. This competition is the plan’s flagged talent signal — P-tuning instinct is the strongest predictor of robotics skill; note on the scorecard who re-tunes between attempts by feel (gain reasoning: lazy vs jittery), not by guessing.
